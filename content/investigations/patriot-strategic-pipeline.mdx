A shell LLC with no website, no employees on record, and one mailbox in a strip-mall UPS Store became — for ninety days last winter — the second-largest source of ad money in a congressional race nobody was supposed to be watching.<Cite id="1" />

Its name was Patriot Strategic LLC. It existed for one quarter. By April, it had been wound down. By May, the candidate it had quietly backed was leading her primary by eleven points.

This is how it worked.

<Chapter n="01" title="One mailbox. Three PACs. Four point two million dollars." id="ch1" />

The Florida Division of Corporations registered Patriot Strategic LLC on November 4, 2025.<Cite id="1" /> The registered agent address was a UPS Store in unincorporated Miami-Dade. The single listed member-manager was a paralegal at a Coral Gables law firm whose name appears on roughly two hundred similar filings going back to 2017.

Within sixty days, three federal independent-expenditure committees — Patriot Forward, Patriot Voices, and Patriot Now — began reporting payments to a single media vendor with consecutive invoice numbers. The total flowing out: **$4,217,400.**<Cite id="2" />

The total flowing in to those same three PACs, in the same window, from a single LLC: **$4,217,400.**<Cite id="3" />

<PullQuote cite="Senior FEC staff attorney, on background">
  When a vendor and a funder share an address, share a routing number, and
  share a launch date, you are not looking at a coincidence. You are looking
  at a structure.
</PullQuote>

The structure is not, by itself, illegal. Limited liability companies can make independent expenditures. PACs can hire vendors. Vendors can buy ad inventory. What is not supposed to happen is the same person sitting on every chair at the table — and the disbursement schedules we obtained suggest that, in this case, somebody did.<Cite id="3" />

<Chapter n="02" title="The Coral Gables paralegal." id="ch2" />

The member-manager listed on the LLC&rsquo;s formation documents is a paralegal who has, by our count, signed off as the named human being on more than two hundred Florida shell entities over the past nine years.<Cite id="1" /> When reached by phone, she declined to discuss specific filings, but said the following:

<PullQuote cite="Paralegal — on the record">
  I sign what the partners ask me to sign. I&rsquo;m the name. I&rsquo;m not
  the money. If you want the money, ask the partners. They won&rsquo;t talk
  to you either.
</PullQuote>

The "partners" — a two-person firm in a glass tower on Brickell Avenue — did not return calls or written questions submitted over a two-week period.<Cite id="5" />

<Aside title="What we know vs. what we&rsquo;re still asking">
  We can document, from public filings, that the money moved. We can document
  that the structure overlapped. What we cannot yet say — and will not say
  until the documents are in our hands — is who, ultimately, wrote the
  checks. A civil complaint filed in the Southern District of Florida in
  March alleges a structure consistent with what we describe here. Pleadings
  are not findings. We are reading them carefully.<Cite id="6" />
</Aside>

<Chapter n="03" title="The race nobody was watching." id="ch3" />

Florida&rsquo;s 27th congressional district covers downtown Miami, parts of Coral Gables, and the western suburbs. It has flipped party in three of the last four cycles. In November 2025, every major polling outfit had it as a tossup. By the end of the Q1 2026 window — when Patriot Strategic LLC went quiet — one candidate was leading the other by eleven points.<Cite id="4" />

The ad money explains some of that. Whether the rest is explained by the candidate, by the moment, or by the structure we describe in this story is a question we are still reporting. The trail we have followed so far is what you have just read. The trail we are following next is in the next chapter, when we publish it.
